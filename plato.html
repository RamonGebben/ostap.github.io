Jeroen Vedder

Owner of Plato, record store on the Voorstraat, Utrecht

“You can say I’m a romantic or just old fashioned when it comes to music, I grew up with Joy Division and Nick Drake before that"

We have an easy stroll through the mellowly lit aisles of Plato admiring their vast collection before being invited join Jeroen in the back room behind the counter. Were surrounded by stacks of vinyls, cds and equipment; clearly a den for true music lovers

So tell us a bit about what you do here

"We find second hand Vinyl’s anywhere we can, but there are a lot of really bad albums from the 70s and 80s so we refuse to sell music we don’t like, they have to have some meaning to us. You will never find Lionel Richie in our second hand albums.

"We have the Audio-technica and soon the Thorens turntables which are good, affordable players that we have high expectations of. We also have a couple of people in town that repair any equipment because it is important that people have a good audio system that enhances your listening experience.

How did you become the owner of Plato?

"I started out in a record shop when I was 16, and I’ve been working in record shops ever since. I have worked in Plato for 25 years now and got the chance to buy the shop a few years ago, we had to do it!

"My partner and I, we just love music and want to share it. I can’t imagine being in a place without being surrounded by records and cds, it’s where I want to be. I like just about everything, Jazz, Classical, just not metal.

There is clearly a love for Vinyl here, what inspires you about the format?

"Vinyl is the thing I grew up with. The funny thing is, when I first started at Plato there was just a tiny section of the last vinyl albums they had left in the shop. Now it’s coming back again and its 40% of our business.

“ It’s something more lasting than cds, I think a lot of young people that buy vinyl are fed up of skipping and shuffling through tracks, so it’s a very relaxing way to listen to music. After twenty minutes you have a feel of the flow of the album, there’s a story in there which makes it worthwhile. There’s one big pressing plant in Holland that bands go to, but usually they come to us to promote cds.

“You can say I’m a romantic or just old fashioned when it comes to music, I grew up with Joy Division and Nick Drake before that. All these tortured people who made just one or two great albums who just disappeared. It’s very different from now with bands that start off with marketing plans. It’s the most realistic route for musicians these days.

“There will always be a market for vinyl and record stores but it will never be how it used to be, an iPod is very handy for the train.
